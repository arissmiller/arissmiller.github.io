Definitions

Independence

  • Two events A and B are independent if the fact that A occurs does not efffect the probability of B occurring

  • Ex. roll a die and get a “6”, roll a second die and get a “3”

  • If A and B are independent, then \(P(A \cup B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)\)

  • If A and B are independent, then \(P(A \mid B) = P(A)\)

Independence and complements

  • If A and B are independent, then \(A^c\) and \(B^c\) are independent

  • \(P(A^c \cup B^c) = P(A \cap B)^c\)

Mutual Exclusion does not imply independence

  • If A and B are mutually exclusive, then the \(P(A \cap B) = 0\)

  • Since \(P(A) \cdot P(B) > 0\) the equality in definition of independence is not satisfied

Independence of more than two events (Mutually Independent Events)

  • Events \(A_1, A_2, A_3, … A_n\) are independent if \(P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_3 … \cap A_n) = P(A_1) \cdot P(A_2) \cdot P(A_3) … \cdot P(A_n)\)

Multiplication rule:

  • If operation A can be done in M different ways, and operation B can be done in n different ways, then the sequence (op A, op B) can be done in m * n different ways.

  • “and” means to multiply, in a sequence of possibilities

Sample space for die: \(S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}\)

Sample space for coin: \(S = {H, T}\)

\(P(H \cup 4) = P(H) \cdot P(4) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1 }{6} =\frac{1}{12}\)

Example 6: Game of Craps: Using 2 dice, you win by rolling a sum of 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10 twice before rolling a sum of 7. What is the probability a player wins with a point of 10?

Ways to sum to 10: \({(4,6), (5,5), (6,4)}\)

Ways to sum to 7: \({(6,1), (5,2), (4,3), (3,4), (2,5), (1,6)}\)

Since all sequences are mutually exclusive, probability of winning a point 10 is the sum of the infitine number of products

\(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{3}{36} \cdot \frac{3}{36} \cdot (\frac{27}{36})^k\)

This is a convergent geometic series

\(\frac{3}{36} \cdot \frac{3}{36} \cdot \frac{1}{1-\frac{27}{36}} = \frac{1}{36}\)
