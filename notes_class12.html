Definitions:

Poisson distribution:

probability mass function that models real world phenomena

  • number of typos on printed page

  • number of cars passing though an intersection

  • alaskan salmon caught in a net

  • cutomsers at an ATM in 10 min intervals

  • number of alpha particles emitted by a radioactive source in a given time

X = descrete ranom variable that denotes the # of times a given event occurs within an interval of time or space

Poisson Limit:

Used to approximate hard to calculate binoomial probabilities where values of n and p reflect conditions of limit

\(n\to \infty\) \(p \to 0\) but \(n \cdot p\) is constant

Remember: \(n \cdot p = E(x)\) for Binomial random variables, and \(n \cdot p \cdot q = Var(x)\)

Modeling a series of events within a given time interval T

  1. Probability >= 2 events occur within a given subinterval isgoes to 0
  2. The events occur indepentently (binomial))
  3. The probability that an event occurs duringa given subinteral is constant over the entire interval from[0 to T]
  4. Each sub interval is like an independent tryial in abinomial model

X = discrete ranom variable for tot al # of vents occuring within T

\(\lambda =\) the rate at which the events are occurring

\(E(X) = \lambda \cdot T = n \cdot p\)

\(P_n =\) probability that an event occurs within a given subinterval = \(\frac{\lambda T}{n}\)

\(\therefore P_x(k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda T}(\lambda T)^k}{k!}\)

The interval between consecutively ocurring events can be modeled with Poisson

Examples

Example 1: Given the random variable X = the number of typos on a printed page, with a mean of 3 typos per page:

a: What is the probability that a randomly selected pag has at least one typo on it?

Use pmf for poisson: \(\frac{e^{-np}(np)^k}{k!} = P(X=k)\)

at least one = 1 - P(X=0))

\(P(X \geq 1)) = 1 - P(X=0) = 1 - \frac{e^{-3}3^0}{0!} = 1 - e^{-3} \approx 0.9502\)

b: What is the probability that randomly selected age will have AT MOST one typo?

at most one: \(P(X \leq 1) = P(X=1) + P(X=0)\)

\(= \frac{e^{-3}3^1}{1!} + \frac{e^{-3}3^0}{0!} = 4e^3 \approx 00.1992\)

The random variable X = the number of bug parts in 200 grams of peanut butter. Assuming the worst contamination level, at the FDA limit of 30 insect parts/100grams (0.3 fragments per gram). what is the proability that a 20 gram sandwich will hae more than 5 bug parts?

In 20 grams of pb, we get (20g)(0.3f) = 6.0 fragments. This means that the mean E(X) = 6.0 = \(\lambda\)

What is the probability that there are 5 or more bug parts?

\(P(X \geq 5) = 1 - P(X \leq 4) = 1 - \sum_{k=0}^4\frac{3^{-6}6^k}{k!} = 1 - 0.29 = 0.71\)

71 % chance of getting 5 or more bug parts in a sandwich
